Old Thomas had tended the lighthouse for forty years. He knew every mood of the sea, every shade of grey in the sky before a storm, and every creak of the spiral staircase that led to the lamp room.

People in the village thought he was lonely. They'd shake their heads and say, "Poor Thomas, all alone up there with nothing but the wind."

But Thomas never felt alone.

---

Every evening, as the sun melted into the horizon, he would climb the one hundred and twelve steps to the top. He'd check the lamp, polish the brass fittings, and then — his favourite part — he'd sit by the window and watch.

He watched the fishing boats return home, their lights bobbing like fireflies on the water. He watched the storms roll in from the west, magnificent and terrible. He watched the stars appear one by one, as if someone was lighting candles in a vast, dark room.

"You see," he once told a visitor, "I'm never alone. The sea tells me stories every night. Different stories, mind you. She never repeats herself."

---

One winter evening, a terrible storm struck the coast. Waves taller than houses crashed against the rocks. The wind howled like a wounded animal. Thomas climbed to the lamp room and found the light had gone out.

His hands were old and shaking, but they knew their work. In the darkness, with the tower swaying beneath him, he relit the lamp. Its beam cut through the storm like a golden sword.

> "We don't keep the light burning for ourselves. We keep it burning for those who are still finding their way home."

The next morning, a fishing boat limped into the harbour. The captain, a young man with salt in his beard and tears in his eyes, came to the lighthouse.

"Your light saved us," he said. "We'd lost all bearings. And then — there it was. Like a star that refused to go out."

Thomas smiled and poured the young man a cup of tea. "That's what lighthouses do," he said simply. "They stay."

---

Thomas tended the lighthouse for three more years after that night. When he finally retired, the whole village came to say goodbye. And every evening, long after he'd moved to a small cottage by the harbour, he would look up at the hill and watch the light sweep across the sea.

The lighthouse stayed. And so did he.
